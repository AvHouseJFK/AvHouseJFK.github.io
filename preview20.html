Aircraft of the Week

Ilyushin IL-76

The Ilyushin Il-76 is a Soviet-designed, four-engine heavy military transport aircraft, known for its robust design and ability to operate from rough airstrips. It is used for carrying troops, equipment, and cargo and has seen both military and civilian service for decades. Key features include its high-wing design, a rear loading ramp, and robust landing gear that can be adjusted for different surfaces.

Field Operations

Runway Dynamics

John F. Kennedy International Airport operates with four runways laid out in two crossing pairs: 4L/22R, 4R/22L, 13L/31R, and 13R/31L. The two 13/31 runways are the longer pair, with 13R/31L stretching over 14,500 feet and serving as the primary heavy departure runway, especially for transatlantic and long-haul flights. 13L/31R is slightly shorter and more commonly used for arrivals. The 4/22 pair is generally shorter and used during different wind conditions, with 4R/22L often handling arrivals and 4L/22R handling departures when in use. Runway use is coordinated by the Port Authority and the FAA, balancing efficiency with noise abatement procedures.

The Canarsie Approach

KJFK has a few unique procedures, most notably the Canarsie Approach to Runway 13L and rarely 13R. This visual approach requires aircraft to follow the Canarsie VOR (CRI) and make a late turn over Jamaica Bay, lining up with the runway at low altitude. It is a challenging arrival that requires visual conditions and is famous among aviation enthusiasts. Most other approaches to JFK are ILS-based and straightforward, with precision instrument guidance to all four main runways. Offset approaches may be used occasionally, particularly for Runway 22L, but these are less common compared to the Canarsie arrival.

Joint Runway Usage & Wind Selection

Because of the airport’s layout, joint usage of runways is common. For example, during heavy traffic periods, JFK will assign 13R for departures while 13L handles arrivals, maximizing efficiency by using parallel flows. Similarly, during operations on the 22s, 22R may be used for departures while 22L takes most arrivals. Runway selection at JFK is highly wind-dependent. Winds from the northwest favor the 31s, southerly winds favor the 22s, easterlies put the 4s into play, and westerlies often favor the 31s. Since JFK sits near the Atlantic coast, winds can shift quickly, and ATC frequently reconfigures traffic flows throughout the day.

The KJFK Chronicle

1939–1948: Planning Idlewild

By the late 1930s, NYC realized LaGuardia was reaching capacity. In 1942, construction began on land previously occupied by the Idlewild Golf Course. On July 1, 1948, the facility officially opened as New York International Airport.

1950s–1963: The Jet Age

Idlewild pioneered the “Terminal City” concept. Pan Am built the Worldport, and TWA opened the iconic Flight Center by Eero Saarinen. In December 1963, following the assassination of President John F. Kennedy, the airport was renamed JFK.

1970s–1980s: Widebody & Concorde

On January 22, 1970, Pan Am launched the first-ever Boeing 747 flight. In 1977, supersonic travel arrived with British Airways and Air France Concorde service, cutting travel to under 3.5 hours.

1990s–2010s: Modernization

JetBlue launched in 1999. In the 2010s, Delta expanded Terminal 4 and the historic TWA Flight Center was transformed into the TWA Hotel, preserving aviation history.

2020s–Present: Mega-Hub Transformation

Major redevelopment projects are reshaping the airport: the $9.5 billion New Terminal One and a new Terminal 6. Today, JFK remains the busiest international gateway in the United States.
